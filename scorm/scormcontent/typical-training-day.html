A typical training day at Flemington

The standard daily training routine for horses at Flemington is repeated for each horse in the stable. They have an intense morning exercise session between 3.30am and 9.30am and a quieter afternoon session between 2.30 pm and 4.30pm. The Strapper puts the horse back in their stable, gives them their main breakfast and puts their rugs on. The stables are mucked out and each horse is dressed – coat, mane and tail brushed and feet oiled.

Note: While doing this, the Strapper looks for any indication of injuries, such as heat swelling or little nicks and checks for loose shoes. If there are any issues, the Strapper reports these to the foreman who will decide with the trainer if they need to bring in the vet or farrier.

Glossary

Term Definition
Beach Work A horse may go to the beach to canter on the sand, wade in the water and may even have a swim!
Break In The education of a young horse to be handled, lead, saddled and ridden in preparation for its racing and post- racing careers.
Breaker A Breaker is a specialised horse person who educates a young horse to be handled, lead, saddled and ridden in preparation for its racing and post-racing careers.
Breeder A Breeder is the person who owns the Mare (Dam) when the foal is born.
Bridle A piece of equipment, which fits on a horse's head and includes a bit and the reins.
Broodmare A mare who is used for breeding.
Clerk of the Course A mounted racing official who monitors and assists all horses getting to the barriers, will accompany fractious horses and will lead the winner of the race into the winners stall.
Colt A male horse three years or younger who has not been castrated.
Dam A horse's female parent is known as its Dam.
Dress A Strapper will ‘dress’ a horse by brushing, oiling feet, putting rugs on and ensuring there are no little cuts etc.
Easy work Same as slow work; a training exercise which may include walking, trotting and cantering at a slow pace.
Entire A non-castrated male horse.
Farrier A Farrier maintains a horse’s hooves. They are responsible for ensuring a horse’s hooves are of good condition, correct shape and are equally balanced. The Farrier also applies the horse’s shoes.
Fast work Training exercise which will include galloping.
Gallop The fastest pace of a horse, with all four feet off the ground together in each stride.
Gelding A male horse which has been castrated.
Gestation period The length of time a mare is in foal. It is 11 months, but can range anywhere from 320-362 days.
Group 1 A race of the highest quality. These races hold the greatest prize money and most importance.
Homebred Retained by the breeder and sent to a trainer to be prepared for the races. In this case the owner is the breeder.
Jockey A Jockey rides the horse in races.
Jump outs An official ‘Jump Out’ where a horse will engage in mini race like conditions for both education and fitness; a trial is recorded on a horses racing ‘form’.
Mare A female horse four years and older, or a female horse who has had a foal.
Muck-out To clean a horse’s stable of droppings and ensure there is an adequate amount of bedding – shavings or straw.
Owner The person who purchases and owns the horse. The owner funds the training and care of the horse during its racing career and will decide what post-racing career path the horse will take.
Retired A racehorse who has ceased its racing career. A ‘retired' horse will go on to participate in one of many different disciplines and careers including breeding, equestrian and pleasure.
Rug To put a horse’s rug on. Horse rugs range from a cotton rug to wool or canvas, depending on the weather and if the horse is stabled inside or outside in a paddock.
Saddle A piece of equipment, which fits on a horse's back and includes stirrups and girth.
Sales yearling Sold by the breeder as a yearling at public auction. The trainer will usually be involved in the selection process.
Sire The male parent of a horse kept for breeding.
Slow work Training exercise which may include walking, trotting and cantering at a slow pace.
Spelling farm A facility where horses will have a break from racing to relax, grow and mature.
Stallion An adult male horse who has not been castrated and is used for breeding purposes, also referred to in racing publications as a 'Sire'.
Steward A raceday official who runs the race meeting and enforces the rules of racing.
Strapper The daily caretaker for the horse whose duties include grooming, feeding, walking, cleaning stables, saddling. This is also the person who leads the horse around the Mounting Yard on raceday.
Stud A facility where the stallions are kept for selective breeding. The owner or breeder will bring the mare to the stud for service (mating) by the stallion they have chosen.
Stud fee The price the owner or breeder will pay for a service by their selected stallion. Each stallion will hold an individual price charged by the stud determined by his breeding/form/progeny.
Syndicate A group of people who buy a share in a racehorse, managed by a syndicate owner. The group will own a percentage of the horse, sharing in both the expenses to train and the winnings earned by the racehorse.
Tack-up To fit the horse with its saddle, bridle and any other pieces of equipment required to engage in trackwork.
Thoroughbred A breed of horse.
Trackrider A person who exercises racehorses on behalf of the trainer so that the horse may be fit and ready for racing.
Trackwork Trackwork is the daily exercise of racehorses; usually occurs between the hours of 3−9am.
Trainer A Trainer is ultimately responsible for the horse’s care whilst in a racing stable, ensuring optimum health and fitness. The Trainer sets the training and nutritional program to ensure the horse is at peak fitness on raceday to be a winning chance.
Treadmill A piece of exercise equipment, similar to a human treadmill but on a larger scale.
Tried Horse Bought via public auction or privately after having at least one preparation, most often having already raced at least once.
Walker A round exercise machine, with partitions dividing multiple horses. Used for warming and cooling horses before and after trackwork and for light afternoon walking.
Yearling A young horse between the ages of one and two years old.
